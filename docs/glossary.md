# Glossary

**Last Updated**: July 02, 2026

## A

**AI-Powered Attacks**: Cyberattacks that leverage artificial intelligence and machine learning to automate reconnaissance, evade detection, or craft more convincing phishing emails.

**Attack Surface**: The total sum of all possible entry points, vulnerabilities, and attack vectors through which an unauthorized user could gain access to a system, network, or data.

**Attack Vector**: The specific pathway, method, or technique that a threat actor uses to gain unauthorized access to a target system (e.g., phishing email, exploited vulnerability, malicious USB drive).

**Authenticity**: One of the principles in the Parkerian Hexad. Refers to the assurance that a piece of data, message, or transaction is genuine and originated from its claimed source.

**Availability**: One of the three pillars of the CIA Triad. Ensures that systems, data, and services are accessible and operational when authorized users need them.

---

## B

**Business World Context**: Understanding that cybersecurity is not just a technical issue but a business risk management function that affects revenue, reputation, regulatory compliance, and customer trust.

---

## C

**Career Opportunities**: The growing demand for cybersecurity professionals across multiple industries, including roles like SOC Analyst, Penetration Tester, Security Consultant, GRC Specialist, and Security Engineer.

**CIA Triad**: The foundational security model consisting of three core principles: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

**Common Threats**: The most frequently encountered cyberattacks including malware, ransomware, phishing, DDoS attacks, supply chain attacks, and insider threats.

**Confidentiality**: One of the three pillars of the CIA Triad. Ensures that sensitive data is only accessible to authorized individuals.

**Control (Possession/Control)**: One of the principles in the Parkerian Hexad. Refers to the ownership and control over data.

**Core Concepts**: The essential mental framework needed to understand cybersecurity, including the CIA Triad, risk management, and the threat landscape.

**Current Threat Trends (2026)**: The evolving threat landscape including AI-powered attacks, ransomware-as-a-service, state-sponsored cyber warfare, supply chain compromises, and IoT/OT attacks.

**Cybersecurity**: The practice of protecting systems, networks, programs, and data from digital attacks, damage, or unauthorized access.

**Cybersecurity vs Information Security vs IT Security**  
- **Cybersecurity**: Focuses on protecting digital systems and networks.  
- **Information Security**: Broader protection of all information assets.  
- **IT Security**: Focuses specifically on securing IT infrastructure.

## D

**DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service)**: A type of attack where multiple compromised systems flood a target with traffic to make it unavailable.

**Defense-in-Depth**: A security strategy that employs multiple layers of defense so that if one layer is breached, others remain effective.

**Definition of Cybersecurity**: The practice of protecting systems, networks, programs, and data from digital attacks.

**Digital Attacks**: Any malicious attempt to compromise, damage, or gain unauthorized access to digital systems.

**Exploitation**: The act of using an exploit to compromise a system.

| **IT Security** | The specific discipline focused on securing information technology infrastructure, including networks, servers, endpoints, databases, and applications, primarily from digital threats. |
| **Landscape (Cybersecurity)** | The collective view of the cybersecurity environment including threats, vulnerabilities, actors, defense mechanisms, regulatory frameworks, and market dynamics. |
| **Least Privilege** | The security principle that users, applications, and processes should only have the minimum level of access and permissions necessary to perform their authorized functions. Reduces the blast radius of a compromise. |
| **Major Players** | The key stakeholders in the cybersecurity ecosystem including: technology vendors (CrowdStrike, Palo Alto, Microsoft), governments (CISA, NSA, NCSC), threat actors (nation-states, cybercrime groups, hacktivists), and industry organizations. |

| **Market Demand** | The high and growing need for cybersecurity professionals and services across all sectors, driven by increasing cyber threats and regulatory requirements. |
| **Mental Framework** | The conceptual understanding and mental model that helps cybersecurity professionals think about security in terms of risks, controls, threats, and vulnerabilities in a structured way. |
| **Mobile Money Fraud** | A specific type of cyber fraud targeting mobile money platforms and digital payment systems, particularly prevalent in regions like Africa where mobile money usage is high. |
| **Networks** | Interconnected systems, devices, and infrastructure that enable communication and data exchange. Cybersecurity protects both the network infrastructure and the data transmitted across it. |
| **New Cyber Threats** | Emerging attack methods and techniques that evolve as technology advances, including AI-generated attacks, ransomware-as-a-service, and cryptocurrency-related attacks. |
| **Parkerian Hexad** | An extension of the CIA Triad that adds three additional security principles: Possession/Control, Authenticity, and Utility. Provides a more comprehensive view of information security. |
| **Payload** | The actual malicious component or code within an attack that performs the intended harmful action, such as data encryption (ransomware), data exfiltration, or system destruction. |
| **Phishing** | A social engineering attack where attackers send deceptive emails, messages, or communications that appear legitimate to trick recipients into revealing sensitive information, clicking malicious links, or opening infected attachments. |
| **Possession/Control** | One of the principles in the Parkerian Hexad. Refers to the ownership, custody, and control over information assets, including the ability to determine who can access, modify, or delete data. |
| **Programs** | Software applications and systems that run on hardware. Cybersecurity protects programs from exploitation, unauthorized modification, and malicious code injection. |
| **Protecting Systems, Networks, Programs, and Data** | The formal definition of what cybersecurity encompasses—the comprehensive protection of all digital assets. |
| **Ransomware** | A type of malware that encrypts a victim's files and demands payment (ransom) in exchange for the decryption key. The most common ransomware variants include LockBit, BlackCat, and REvil. |
| **Ransomware Evolution** | The progression of ransomware attacks from simple encryption to double-extortion (stealing data before encrypting), triple-extortion, and ransomware-as-a-service business models. |
| **Realistic Expectations** | The understanding of what a cybersecurity career entails—including the challenges, learning curve, need for continuous education, and the rewarding nature of protecting organizations. |
| **Risk** | The combination of a threat exploiting a vulnerability to cause an impact. Formula: Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Impact. |
| **Risk Management** | The process of identifying, assessing, and prioritizing risks, followed by implementing strategies to mitigate, transfer, accept, or avoid them. |
| **Scope of Cybersecurity** | The breadth of cybersecurity practice including all digital assets, systems, networks, data, and the people who interact with them. |
| **Security (Cybersecurity)** | The state of being protected from digital threats, attacks, and unauthorized access. |
| **Social Engineering** | The psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. Common techniques include phishing, pretexting, baiting, and tailgating. |
| **Spyware** | A type of malware that secretly monitors user activity, captures keystrokes, and collects sensitive information without the user's knowledge. |
| **State-Sponsored Threats** | Cyberattacks conducted by or on behalf of nation-states, often for espionage, political influence, disruption, or military advantage. Considered highly sophisticated and well-resourced. |
| **Strong Mental Framework** | A solid, foundational understanding of cybersecurity concepts that enables professionals to think critically about security challenges and apply first principles. |
| **Supply Chain Attack** | A cyberattack that exploits vulnerabilities in an organization's supply chain, vendors, or third-party partners to compromise the primary target. Examples include the SolarWinds and Kaseya attacks. |
| **System Protection** | The practice of securing computer systems, servers, devices, and infrastructure from unauthorized access, attacks, and damage. |
| **Telecom Attacks** | Cyberattacks targeting telecommunications infrastructure, including mobile networks, fiber optic cables, switching centers, and communication providers. Particularly relevant in African contexts. |
| **Threat** | Any entity, event, or action that has the potential to cause harm, damage, or disruption to a system, data, or organization. |
| **Threat Landscape** | The overall picture of all cyber threats, attackers, and attack techniques that organizations face, including their prevalence, sophistication, and evolution over time. |
| **Threat Vulnerability Impact** | The three components of risk: A threat exploits a vulnerability causing an impact. Understanding this relationship is fundamental to cybersecurity. |
| **Utility** | One of the principles in the Parkerian Hexad. Refers to the usefulness and usability of data—data must be in a format and condition that makes it valuable and usable to authorized users. |
| **Virus** | A type of malware that replicates itself by inserting its code into other programs, files, or boot sectors. Unlike worms, viruses require user action (like opening an infected file) to propagate. |
| **Vulnerability** | A weakness, flaw, or gap in a system, application, network, or process that could be exploited by a threat to cause harm or unauthorized access. |
| **Vulnerability vs Virus (Difference)** | A virus is a type of malware that can cause damage. A vulnerability is a weakness that allows threats (including viruses) to cause harm. The distinction is important for risk analysis. |
| **Zero Trust** | A security model that operates on the principle of "never trust, always verify." It assumes no user, device, or network is trustworthy by default, even if inside the network perimeter. Requires continuous authentication and authorization. |
| **Zero-Day** | A vulnerability that is unknown to the software vendor or security community. Since there is no patch available, zero-day vulnerabilities are considered extremely dangerous and valuable to attackers. |
